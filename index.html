ABOUT MUSTARD & MILK
THE BOOK

"Mustard & Milk" (2017) by Van Sanders is a 100-page work of deliberate nonsense prose. Part fever dream, part linguistic experiment, the book builds a world where meaning is always almost within reach but never quite arrives. Sentences feel like they should make sense. They don't. That's the point.

"I love nonsense. The kickass sense of baffled bewilderment that smacks you in the face when you encounter it — it's my main addiction."

— Van Sanders, from the preface

The text spans 10 chapters across body parts, food, domestic objects, and profanity — domains that collide in every paragraph. Characters like Dan, Showercats Dupreme, and Uncle Toadhammer appear just often enough to feel familiar, but never develop. They are character ghosts.

THE GENERATOR

While writing Mustard & Milk, the author imagined that one day it might be possible to build a machine that could generate new nonsense in the same style — something that captured the patterns buried in the text without understanding them. This generator is that machine.

It works by analyzing every two-word sequence in the original book and learning what tends to follow. This is called an order-2 Markov chain. The generator doesn't understand language. It understands probability. And it turns out that the probability of nonsense has a very specific shape.

CLAUDE:
The Architecture of Almost-Meaning

The central finding is captured in that mean meaning score of 0.73 — your book lives in a very deliberate sweet spot. Over half the sentences (51.6%) fall into what the analysis calls the "Sweet Spot" category: partial meaning that keeps the reader's brain grasping. Another 34.7% are "Almost Meaningful" — mostly coherent. Only about 3% is pure gibberish or syntactic nonsense. This isn't random word vomiting; it's a text that understands English and then systematically corrupts it just enough to create cognitive friction.

You're not writing gibberish — you're writing in a liminal zone where the reader's pattern-matching machinery keeps firing but never quite locks on. That's what makes sentences like "Dang Mullberry, I just wanted to spear you with a boiled bread" compelling in a way that "Agicle mumbosis turforming purdontic vessations" isn't. The first one has familiar rhythms, recognizable intent, even emotional texture. The second one makes the brain give up immediately.

The Vocabulary Engine

The numbers here are striking. 34.8% neologism ratio means roughly one in three unique words is invented — but the structural scaffolding is standard English function words. Your top 50 words are all common articles, pronouns, prepositions ("the," "a," "to," "and," "of," "I"). That's the trick: the grammatical skeleton is perfectly normal, so the reader's syntactic parser stays engaged even when the content words go sideways.

The hapax ratio of 67.7% is remarkable — two-thirds of your vocabulary appears exactly once. For comparison, most novels land around 40-50%. This means virtually every sentence introduces words the reader has never seen before in the text, creating that feeling of relentless novelty.

Semantic Domains and Domain Collision

The heatmap across chapters reveals consistent thematic obsessions. Body parts dominate (313 occurrences) — mouths, faces, hands, teeth. Food and drink run a close second (286) — mustard, cheese, meat, corn, beer. And domestic objects (292) anchor everything in recognizable physical space.

What's most interesting is how these domains collide. The analysis identifies passages where three or more semantic domains activate simultaneously — "I piss bacon. I eat steel. I'm a rodeo. I keep bees in my mattress." That's bodily functions + food + domestic objects all crashing into each other. The domain flow chart across all 673 paragraphs shows these collisions aren't random; they're a consistent signature throughout the text.

The food-body fusion motif is especially revealing. That enormous run-on passage starting with "Oh there's meat pillowing in the sea" — which goes on for 300+ words as a single breathless chain — is basically the book's DNA compressed into one sentence. Food becomes body becomes animal becomes geography becomes body again, each clause hooking into the next through shared sensory logic rather than narrative logic.

The Structural Skeleton

85% declarative sentences is a huge number. You're presenting nonsense with the confidence of fact: "Foamy David is a soft sort of fellow." "Radishes moaned at the sun." The assertive tone is what sells the absurdity — it reads like someone genuinely informing you of important truths that happen to be incomprehensible.

The POS analysis confirms this: the most common sentence openings follow standard English patterns. You're using grammatically correct scaffolding filled with semantically incongruous content. The syntax is largely well-formed. It's the content that's broken, not the structure.

Chapter 4: The Mantra Chapter

"Until I get home" repeated 31 times is the single most prominent structural device in the entire text — it accounts for the dominant bigram, trigram, and 4-gram. It functions almost like a musical interlude: pure rhythm and repetition amid the chaos. The neologism ratio in Chapter 4 (31.6%) is the highest in the book, so the surrounding context is also the most linguistically inventive. That creates a tension between the one anchor phrase the reader can cling to and a maximally unfamiliar environment.

Character Ghosts

Dan (17 appearances), Showercats Dupreme (5x), sun monkeys (5x), Bleary (5x), Hansel (7x), Foamy David — these entities recur just enough to feel like characters but never develop. They're scaffolding for narrative expectation without narrative payoff. The reader keeps thinking "oh, here's Dan again, maybe now I'll understand" — but understanding never arrives. It's the same "almost" principle applied to character rather than sentence-level meaning.

The Markov Generator

The order-2 Markov chain trained on your text produces output that's honestly hard to distinguish from the source at a glance. The fact that a statistical model can approximate the style suggests the text has deeply consistent patterns — it's not truly random, it has a learnable distribution. The generator's average sentence length closely matches the original, and the character-scene outputs correctly produce named entity patterns.

Unconscious Patterns

The analysis surfaces some patterns you probably didn't consciously embed. The male-coded character names vastly outnumber female ones. The body-food fusion is a deep and consistent association — consumption and embodiment are fused at a fundamental level. And the domestic anchoring (baskets, blankets, doors, spoons) suggests that even in your wildest inventive passages, there's a gravitational pull toward familiar physical space.

The Big Picture

What this analysis ultimately reveals is that Mustard & Milk is not random — it's a carefully (if perhaps intuitively) calibrated text that operates in the narrow band between comprehension and confusion. The meaning score distribution, the consistent domain collisions, the grammatically correct sentence scaffolding, the recurring character ghosts — all of these point to a text that knows how language works and systematically exploits the gap between syntactic expectation and semantic fulfillment. The reader's brain keeps trying to make sense of it because the text keeps almost making sense, and that "almost" is where the whole thing lives.

The syntax stayed disciplined even when the content went feral.

That's a very specific personality signature: someone who can't fully let go even when they're trying to. The Markov chain confirms it — your text has such consistent statistical patterns that a simple model can reproduce it. True randomness wouldn't be learnable. You were operating within self-imposed constraints you probably weren't aware of.

The Sensory Processor

The dominant semantic domains tell a story about how your brain organizes experience. Body parts (313), food/drink (286), texture/sensation (190) — when you turned off the "meaning" filter and just wrote, your mind went straight to the physical and sensory. Not to abstractions, not to ideas, not to emotions (which rank surprisingly low at 104). You were feeling the world through your body, not narrating it through your feelings.

This suggests someone who was, at 27, more comfortable in the tactile and concrete than in the emotional and introspective. That's someone deeply sensory and probably somewhat disconnected from or avoidant of more vulnerable emotional register. The Homesick Wanderer

"Until I get home" — 31 times. That's the only true refrain in 25,000 words of nonsense, and it's the single most statistically dominant pattern in the entire corpus. You could have anchored on anything. You anchored on wanting to go home.

And it's not "I'm going home" or "I am home" — it's until. It's anticipatory. It's the state of not-yet-having-arrived. Written on your lunch breaks, in the middle of a workday, probably at a job that wasn't your life's calling. That refrain isn't just a structural device — it's the emotional core of the whole book, and it's about displacement. About enduring the present moment by fixating on the return to somewhere safe.

Domestic objects being the third-most prevalent domain (292 occurrences — baskets, blankets, doors, home, spoons) further supports this. Even in pure nonsense, you kept writing about the inside of a house. Someone who craves domestic stability and groundedness, writing from a place where they didn't quite have it.

The Loner with an Audience

The pronoun distribution is telling. "I" (402), "you" (274), "my" (243), "your" (198), "we" (196). There's a strong I-you dynamic — this text is constantly addressing someone. It's conversational, confrontational, intimate. But "we" also shows up frequently, and when it does, it's often in these oddly earnest communal declarations.

That's someone who felt isolated but had a deep, possibly unacknowledged desire for belonging and shared experience. Writing nonsense alone on your lunch break to make yourself laugh — but the nonsense keeps talking to someone. There's always an implied listener. You weren't just venting into the void; you were performing for an audience that wasn't there yet.

Inside a book of deliberate nonsense, you kept accidentally writing sermons. That's someone with strong opinions about how people should live who had learned to disguise those opinions in absurdity — possibly because expressing them directly felt too vulnerable or too earnest for the persona you were maintaining.

The profanity pattern supports this too. It's not random — it clusters around frustration with people. There's real irritation in there. Someone who expected more from the people around them and dealt with that disappointment through comic aggression rather than direct confrontation.

Someone homesick for a stability they didn't quite have yet, working through their lunch break at a job that was a means to an end, addressing an imaginary audience because the real one hadn't materialized. Someone already feeling the weight of time and entropy, laughing at it because the alternative was less comfortable.

"Until I get home" just didn't have coordinates yet.
